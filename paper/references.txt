================================================================================
## 1. Prompt Injection Attacks (Foundational)
================================================================================

[greshake2023] Greshake et al.
"Not what you've signed up for: Compromising Real-World LLM-Integrated Applications
with Indirect Prompt Injection"
arXiv 2023. arXiv:2302.12173
-- Seminal paper defining indirect prompt injection; attacks on Bing Chat and
   code-completion engines. Key citation for establishing the attack class.

[liu2023houyi] Liu et al.
"Prompt Injection attack against LLM-integrated Applications"
arXiv 2023. arXiv:2306.05499
-- Formalizes prompt injection; introduces HouYi black-box attack technique
   inspired by web injection. Closest prior formal treatment of the attack.

[liu2024universal] Liu et al.
"Automatic and Universal Prompt Injection Attacks against Large Language Models"
arXiv 2024. arXiv:2403.04957
-- Automated gradient-based universal payloads. Relevant to our attack
   optimization component.

[yi2023bipia] Yi et al.
"Benchmarking and Defending against Indirect Prompt Injection Attacks on Large
Language Models"
ACM SIGKDD 2024.
-- BIPIA benchmark; shows more capable models are more susceptible. Motivates
   our cross-provider comparison.

================================================================================
## 2. Prompt Injection in LLM Agents / Tool-Calling
================================================================================

[zhan2024injecagent] Zhan, Liang et al.
"InjecAgent: Benchmarking Indirect Prompt Injections in Tool-Integrated Large
Language Model Agents"
ACL Findings 2024. arXiv:2403.02691
-- 1,054 test cases; GPT-4 ReAct agents vulnerable 24% of the time. Closest
   prior benchmark to ours but uses simulated tool calls.

[perez2022ignore] Perez & Ribeiro
"Ignore Previous Prompt: Attack Techniques For Language Models"
NeurIPS ML Safety Workshop 2022. arXiv:2211.09527
-- Foundational short paper coining the "ignore previous prompt" attack pattern.

================================================================================
## 3. Security Benchmarks for LLM Agents
================================================================================

[debenedetti2024agentdojo] Debenedetti, Zhang et al.
"AgentDojo: A Dynamic Environment to Evaluate Attacks and Defenses for LLM Agents"
NeurIPS Datasets & Benchmarks 2024. arXiv:2406.13352
-- Primary reference benchmark. 97 tasks, 629 security cases, simulated
   environments. Key contrast: we use real execution, not simulation.

[ruan2023toolemu] Ruan et al.
"Identifying the Risks of LM Agents with an LM-Emulated Sandbox"
ICLR 2024. arXiv:2309.15817
-- ToolEmu uses LM-based tool emulation. Relevant contrast: LM simulation
   of tool calls vs. our real execution.

[shi2024agentharm] Shi et al.
"AgentHarm: A Benchmark for Measuring Harmfulness of LLM Agents"
ICLR 2025. arXiv:2410.09024
-- Evaluates harmful behaviors in agents. Related scope but focuses on
   misuse rather than adversarial injection.

================================================================================
## 4. CI/CD and Software Supply Chain Security
================================================================================

[koishybayev2022characterizing] Koishybayev et al.
"Characterizing the Security of Github CI Workflows"
USENIX Security 2022.
-- Systematic security analysis of GitHub Actions workflows (permission
   misuse, secret exposure). Most relevant prior academic CI/CD security work.

[enck2022github] Enck & Williams
"Top Four Open Source Software Security Challenges"
IEEE Security & Privacy 2022.
-- Frames CI/CD as a critical software supply chain attack surface.

[ladisa2023sok] Ladisa et al.
"SoK: Taxonomy of Attacks on Open-Source Software Supply Chains"
IEEE S&P 2023.
-- Systematization of supply chain attacks. Provides framing for our
   integrity compromise attack category.

================================================================================
## 5. Defenses Against Prompt Injection
================================================================================

[hines2024defending] Hines et al.
"Defending Against Indirect Prompt Injection Attacks with Spotlighting"
arXiv 2024. arXiv:2403.14720
-- Data delimiting defense (spotlighting). Directly relevant to the
   prompt-level defenses we compare against workflow-level defenses.

[chen2024struq] Chen et al.
"StruQ: Defending Against Prompt Injection with Structured Queries"
arXiv 2024. arXiv:2402.06363
-- Structured query defense separating instructions from data. Relevant
   to our defense taxonomy.

[debenedetti2024agentdojo] (same as above — also evaluates 4 defenses)

================================================================================
## 6. Jailbreaking (brief mention — related but distinct attack class)
================================================================================

[chao2024jailbreakbench] Chao et al.
"JailbreakBench: An Open Robustness Benchmark for Jailbreaking Large Language Models"
NeurIPS Datasets & Benchmarks 2024. arXiv:2404.01318
-- Standard jailbreak benchmark. Cited to distinguish prompt injection
   (adversary controls data, not user turn) from jailbreaking.

[zou2023gcg] Zou et al.
"Universal and Transferable Adversarial Attacks on Aligned Language Models"
arXiv 2023. arXiv:2307.15043
-- GCG attack. Foundational adversarial NLP paper; cited as contrast
   (white-box vs. our black-box threat model).
